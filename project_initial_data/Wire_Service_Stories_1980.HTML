Private Firms Hired To Help Restore Financial Order


BYLINE: By ROBERT L. SHAFFER, Associated Press Writer


SECTION: Domestic News


LENGTH: 299 words


DATELINE: CLEVELAND


Fourteen months after Cleveland defaulted on $14 million worth of bank loans, city officials say the city's finances are still in such a chaotic state they cannot be audited.

Recently appointed City Finance Director William J. Reidy says a private company has been hired to install a computerized system which he hopes will bring order and help restore confidence in the city among the financial community. 

Reporting Wednesday to the commission created by the state to supervise Cleveland's finances, Reidy said he hopes that by the end of the year the new system will provide "better financial information than I am able to give you today."

Reidy said the system will be developed by the accounting firm of Peat, Marwick, Mitchell & Co., which has installed similar systems in San Francisco, Philadelphia, San Diego, and Columbus, Ohio.

Peat, Marwick is one of three private firms that have been hired to help bring order to Cleveland's finances and rebuild the city's credibility in the financial community.

Reidy said the accounting system was "the sort of thing companies like Lazard Freres (named earlier this week as an investment counselor) will be insisting on" before the city's credit rating would be restored to where it was a few years ago.

The financial planning and supervision commission, holding its second meeting in City Hall, named as its financial supervisor for Cleveland the nationally-known accounting firm of Deloitte, Haskins & Sells.

James M. Delaney, partner-in-charge of Deloitte's Cleveland office, said the company will review the city's financial recovery plan for the commission, and see that a balanced budget is prepared and that the city restores money to special funds that have been raided over a period of years to keep the city operating.

Prosecutors To Question Bert Lance's Relatives Who Got Big Loans


BYLINE: By CHARLES CAMPBELL, Associated Press Writer


SECTION: Domestic News


LENGTH: 359 words


DATELINE: ATLANTA


Bert Lance's mother-in-law and sister-in-law, who got big bank loans, are scheduled to appear as government witnesses this week in the former federal budget director's bank fraud trial.

Prosecutors have served notice they consider the two women -- Ruth Chance and Marguerite David -- to be "hostile witnesses" and will ask permission to cross-examine them.

As the federal court trial enters its 10th week, prosecutors are finally getting into the meat of their charges against Lance and three associates. 

Once a top adviser to President Carter, Lance is charged with conspiracy, misuse of bank funds, false statements to banks and false entries in bank records in connection with hundreds of loans totaling more than $20 million.

Two counts in the 33-count indictment allege misapplication of funds in connection with a pair of loans to Lance's in-laws on May 7, 1975.

On that day Mrs. Chance, mother of Lance's wife, LaBelle, borrowed $100,000 from the Northwest Georgia Bank in Ringgold, where Lance was a major stockholder, one of his co-defendants was president and another defendant was on the committee that approved major loans.

Northwest Georgia made an $80,000 loan the same day to Mrs. David, wife of LaBelle's brother, Claude Barker David.

According to previous testimony at the trial, the proceeds of both loans were issued as cashier's checks made out to the Calhoun First National Bank, where Lance had been president for a decade and was chairman of the board.

The money was used to cover overdrafts at Calhoun First National in the checking accounts of the two women and two of LaBelle Lance's brothers, Claude Barker David and Beverly Banks David, according to testimony.

A federal bank regulator, Richard Newell, was conducting a routine examination of the Calhoun bank at the time. Newell testified that he insisted that the overdrafts be repaid or written off the bank's books before he left the premises of the bank.

Lance's co-defendants are Richard Carr, former president at Northwest Georgia Bank; Thomas Mitchell, once a member of the bank's executive committee; and Jack Mullins, a former Calhoun pharmacist.

The prospectus said Ford had "entered into Credit Agreements with various banks which provide that Ford may borrow, repay, and reborrow up to an aggregate of $1 billion outstanding at any one time. Any borowings ... must be repaid not later than Dec. 31, 1984." 

Ford also has more familiar arrangements with banks to borrow another $1 billion under what the prospectus called short-term lines of credit.

"Short-term" generally means less than a year. Ford did borrow money under those lines last fall when it had shipped new models to dealers but had not been paid for them.

No Ford spokesman was immediately available to discuss the new bank arrangements.

Mary Ann Keller, auto industry analyst for the New York brokerage firm of Paine Webber, Mitchell, Hutchins & Co. Inc., said when asked if the new credit line represented a vote of confidence in Ford by the banks, "I don't think votes of confidence have to be taken. Not every corporation is a Chrysler."

Chrysler Corp. and its banks have been negotiating for months about new and renewal financing. Chrysler must raise $2 billion from various sources in order to qualify for the $1.5 billion in loan guarantees voted by Congress -- a job it hopes to complete by the end of March.

Mrs. Keller said Ford's arrangement had been general knowledge in the financial community even though nothing had been printed about it. "You can't line up a billion dollars without people knowing about it."

Ford did not mention the agreement in its year-end statement given to reporters.

Mrs. Keller said the new bank credit could be viewed as a reserve for capital expenditures if capital market conditions were temporarily unfavorable for raising money through an issue of securities at some time in the future.

Ford would have to pay "prime rate or prime-plus" to the banks while its notes probably can be sold at an interest rate below that, she said.

NYC Bank Loans Drop Sharply, M1A off $100 Million


BYLINE: By WILLIAM GLASGALL, AP Busine


SECTION: Business News


LENGTH: 569 words


Business lending dropped by more than a half-billion dollars at major banks here as the nation's basic money supply fell $100 million in the week ended March 26, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York said Friday.

But recent anti-inflation moves by the Federal Reserve and the Carter administration, which have pushed interest rates to record highs, may not yet have curbed businesses' appetite for borrowed money, analysts said.

The New York Fed said business loans at major banks here fell by $552 million in the latest week, against a gain of $232 million a week before. 

The decline "more than offsets the gains in the previous week," and with "complete March data now recorded, seasonally-adjusted loans posted their first monthly decline in more than a year," said Bankers Trust Co. economist Jay N. Woodworth.

But at the brokerage house of Salomon Brothers, economist Henry Kaufman said he believes business loan activity "is by no means languid."

Reluctance by corporations to enter a depressed bond market "when financing requirements are particularly heavy is causing increasing demands" on short-term borrowing markets, he said, noting, "the demand for short-term business credit has not yet abated."

Kaufman, in a report issued Friday before the weekly Federal Reserve statistics were announced, said, "thus far, it appears that business borrowers who were willing to ay market rates of interest are still finding credit available.

"The head-to-head confrontation between squeezed bank lenders and aggressive business borrowers are still ahead of us -- though the prime loan rate has already moved up to 20 percent," he said.

As one sign of pressure on the banking system, the New York Fed said the rate on federal funds, short-term loans of uncommitted reserves among banks, jumped to a record 19.39 percent in the latest week from 17.78 percent a week earlier.

The New York Fed said M1A, comprising cash in circulation and most checking deposits at commercial banks, fell $100 million to $374.7 billion, while the broader monetary measure M1B also fell $100 million to $391.6 billion.

M1B comprises M1A and checking-type deposits at all financial institutions.

The Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis said, meanwhile, that the monetary base rose $400 million to $157.4 billion. The base is the seasonally-adjusted total of member bank reserves held at Federal Reserve banks and cash in bank vaults and in circulation.

In the latest four weeks, M1A averaged $375.1 billion and grew at a 4.5 percent annual rate from 13 weeks before, while M1B averaged $392 billion and grew at a 5.1 percent-a-year rate.

The Federal Reserve, fearing too-rapid monetary growth will feed inflation, is aiming for an annual growth rate in 1980 of 3.5 to 6 percent for M1A.

Other monetary indicators for the week ended March 26 included:

Commercial banks' net borrowed reserves totaled $1.607 billion, against $2.315 billion a week before.

The Fed reduced its holdings of government securities by $4.027 billion. Its holdings of federal agency issues were unchanged.

Member bank borrowings from the Federal Reserve System fell to $2.097 billion from $2.483 billion.

The average rate paid by major banks on large certificates of deposits rose to 17.97 percent from 17.63 percent a week before.

The three-month Treasury bill rate averaged 14.80 percent, off from 15.61 percent a week earlier.

As of April 11, General Public Utilities had outstanding loans of $54 million under the agreement, while Jersey Central Power & Light had $80 million outstanding and Met-Ed, $92 million outstanding. These total $226 million of the $292 million loan. 

Monday's filing with the New Jersey Board of Public Utilities results from meetings this week with the banks involved. the meetings had been requested by the banks as a result of recent New Jersey Board of Public Utilities action removing unit 1 of the Three Mile Island Nuclear Generating Station from New Jersey Central Power & Light's base rates.

Unit 1, which was out of service for refueling when the March 28, 1979, accident occurred at Three Mile Island Unit 2, was undamaged in the accident. Its return to service, however, has been delayed by procedures imposed by the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission, which contemplates public hearings to begin later this year.

The bank meetings resulted in a letter from the banks to the General Public Utilities companies received today. In the letter the banks reconfirmed their previously announced position that no request to increase the laon limit above the present $292 million would be considered by the banks until there is greater assurance of the borrowers' on-going financial viability, including favorable regulatory action.

The banks also indicated that no increases would be granted in the loan limits of the individual General Public Utilities companies.

The banks agree to permit Jersey Central Power & light to borrow an additional $30 million without any additional collateral. The banks further agreed that, with the receipt of the New Jersey Board of Public Utilities approval for Jersey Central Power & Light to pledge its accounts receivable, Jersey Central Power & Light would be permitted to borrow up to its initial limit of $139 million, but emphasized the importance of near-term restoration of earnings to permit the company to have access to the long-term capital markets.

The action by the banks came after Jersey Central Power & Light pointed out that it would require approximately $60 million of additional borrowings by the end of May with about half that amount needed by May 1.

The borrowing need results from several large expenditures including fuel, purchased power, interest, and preferred stock dividends, and state tax payments.

Included are tax payments due to the state of New Jersey of $9 million May and $22 million May 15, which are required earlier as a result of a recent change in the tax law.

The banks expect that the indebtedness of Met-Ed under the credit agreement will be limited to $110 million until May 15, 1980, and $105 million subsequent to May 15, 1980.

The banks acknowledged that the Pennsylvania Puc's initial decisions in the pending proceeding involving Met-Ed are not scheduled to be issued until May 9, 1980, and that such decisions may allay certain of the concerns of the banks.

However, in view of the recent action by the New Jersey Board of Public Utilities and of projected near-term borrowings by each of Jersey Central Power & Light and Met-Ed, the banks believe that prudence requires an immediate statement.

The company indicated that Jersey Central Power & Light will file sometime this month for an increase in base rates. The amount of the increase to be sought in the filing has not yet been determined.

Consumer Watch: Insurance You May Not Know You Have


BYLINE: By LOUISE COOK, Associated Press Writer


SECTION: Domestic News


LENGTH: 631 words


If you've ever borrowed money or signed a credit agreement, you may have gotten -- and paid for -- more than you expected.

Many loan contracts include a provision for life insurance and some consumer activists say borrowers often don't realize they are buying the extra protection.

The purpose of the insurance is to make sure the creditor gets paid if the borrower dies and to protect the borrower's survivors against debts.

You are probably familiar with this type of insurance if you own a house; lenders frequently require the purchase of a separate life insurance policy as a condition for a mortgage. You may not realize, however, that credit insurance also is offered for small loans and credit agreements. 

The American Council on Life Insurance estimated that as of the end of 1979, credit life insurance accounted for 5 percent of all life insurance in the United States. Other sources estimate that Americans pay anywhere from $1 billion to $3 billion a year in premiums on credit insurance policies.

Credit insurance was introduced in the United States in 1917. At that time, the cost generally was absorbed by the lender. Today, however, the price of the policy usually is passed on to the customer. Most credit insurance is charged to the borrower, in full, when the loan or credit agreement is signed. A lump sum, representing the total of all the premiums during the term of the loan, is added to the amount of the debt. And finance charges are applied to the premiums -- just as they are to the loan itself.

Federal regulations require lenders to disclose finance charges for credit life insurance only if the purchase of the insurance is mandatory. When the purchase is voluntary -- as it is for most smaller loans -- the lender may not necessarily explain the added charges.

The Paul Douglas Consumer Research Center, the education and research arm of the Consumer Federation of America, studied credit insurance several years ago and concluded: "Because the monthly credit insurance premium charge added to most credit transactions ... is relatively small and because most consumers usually sign the authorization for it when a salesperson tells them to, without asking questions, many people who purchase the insurance are hardly aware of having done so."

Credit insurance can be a valuable protection. On a small loan, however, it may not be worth the added expense. Your existing insurance may be adequate. If you need additional coverage, you may be able to get a separate policy from someone other than the lender at a lower price. Find out exactly how much different lenders charge for credit insurance, including interest, and compare costs.

There are two basic types of credit insurance -- credit life, the most widespread, and credit disability. With credit life insurance, your life is insured for the amount of the debt. If you die, the benefits are paid directly to the creditor. Most credit life policies are decreasing-term insurance. As time progresses and the amount of the loan still outstanding gets smallr, the amount of protection drops. Unlike regular life insurance rates, premiums for credit life insurance usually do not vary according to age.

Credit disability policies provide for the insurer to make loan payments during periods when the borrower is disabled. In most cases, this kind of coverage is available only as a supplement to credit life insurance. Creditors generally offer either "retroactive" or "elimination" plans. In both cases, there is a waiting period. You must be disabled for a fixed amount of time -- usually 14 or 30 days -- before benefits will be paid. With retroactive policies, back payments will be made once the waiting period has passed. With elimination policies, there are no back payments.

Federal election law provides full campaign funding for major party nominees, and says minor party or new party candidates may get a proportionate share of federal funds if they get at least 5 percent of the presidential vote.

ConsumerWatch: Gobbledygook Must Go


BYLINE: By LOUISE COOK, Associated Press Writer


SECTION: Domestic News


LENGTH: 633 words


Word by word, clause by clause and sentence by sentence, the fine print in legal documents is getting easier to understand.

Insurance policies refer to "you" and "we" instead of the "undersigned" and the "party of the first part."

Credit agreements warn of what happens if you don't pay your bills on time, instead of "upon failure to make any payment herein agreed."

Even the federal government is trying to make its regulations shorter and simpler. 

The "Plain English" movement began about a decade ago. The insurance industry developed standardized forms for auto and homeowner policies. Banks started to review their loan applications -- and discovered that some of the language was not only confusing, but meaningless.

By the end of the 1970s, the revised insurance forms were in use in almost three-fourths of the 50 states. The new policies have only about half as many words as the earlier ones; type is larger and there is more space between the lines.

New York became the first state to pass a broad-based "Plain English" law, covering not only insurance forms but a wide range of other documents. The 1978 act requires all consumer contracts for less than $50,000 to use words with "common and everyday" meanings. Connecticut, Maine and Hawaii now have similar laws, and the Council of State Governments reports that the idea is under consideration in more than a dozen other states.

Critics of the New York law complain that by limiting the dollar value of contracts to $50,000, the Legislature automatically exempted most real estate transactions. They also say the penalties -- fines of up to $10,000 -- are not stiff enough.

The New York attorney general's office has brought only one suit under the "Plain English" law -- a challenge to the readability of rules and regulations issued by the Lincoln Savings Bank for holders of safe deposit boxes. The case was settled, without penalty, when the bank agreed to change its forms.

Nathan Riley, a spokesman for the attorney general, said the 2 1/2 -year-old statute has helped consumers. "I think that the law has made the agreements more accessible to people," he said. Riley noted, however, that the law can do nothing to force consumers to read what they sign. It is up to the individual buyer to take the first step.

Plain Talk Inc., a non-profit organization set up in Washington, D.C., a little more than a year ago to encourage the use of simple language, has prepared a model law, now under consideration in the District of Columbia.

Ramsey Seldin, president of Plain Talk, said the model statute would cover all consumer contracts, small business agreements and district regulations. It would apply to all consumer contracts involving amounts up to $100,000 and to all consumer real estate transactions, regardless of price. Violators would face a $50,000 penalty.

Seldin said there have been some improvements at the state level. He said, for example, that Virginia lawmakers are considering a bill to make insurance contracts easier to read and a "Plain English" task force has been set up in Maryland.

At the federal level, Seldin said, "there are some real success stories," notably with the Civil Aeronautics Board. And he noted that the Internal Revenue Service is striving to make commonly used federal tax forms less complicated.

Reformers still face one problem: How simple is simple enough?

Some states rely on readability formulas, counting words and syllables to decide whether something can be understood by a person of a given age or with a specific amount of education. Seldin said, however, that formulas should be used only in conjunction with other tests -- by the kind of people who will actually have to read and understand the forms, and by panels of educators and other experts.

It was no laughing matter to Sanders, who Wednesday night resigned after 10 weeks as city manager.

Sanders said wanted no part "of a government that refuses to apply a legal remedy to making itself fiscally sound."

Sanders took over from Ron Stack, who resigned as city manager in August after telling the city it faced a $600,000 deficit at the end of the fiscal year on Sept. 30.

Reports on file with state officials in Tallahassee show Stack failed to tell the city commission about the growing debt, which accumulated over three years. Commissioners declared the city insolvent after Stack's resignation, but later rescinded the action.

In his parting report to the commission, Sanders listed his laments:

The city violates state health standards and creates a fire hazard because it can't afford to buy top soil to cover its trash. The supplier won't even take cash until the city pays its $100,000 dirt debt.

The city was able to meet its payroll last week only because officials hastily negotiated the bank loan. Delayed tax collections were largely to blame but the city debt added to the problem.

The commission refused to enact a 10 percent utility tax proposed by Sanders to raise some $500,000 in additional revenue a year.

"I have six or seven creditors call me a day whom I have no answers to," Sanders told the commissioners Monday night, when he warned that he might quit.

The commissioners appointed Robert Bensko of Boca Raton, Fla., as interim city manager.

"She has reluctantly agreed to such a thing," McCoy said.

DATELINE: DETROIT


Chrysler Corp. said Tuesday it has completed work on its application for another $400 million in federal loan guarantees and is submitting it to the government.

In Washington, a spokesman for the Chrysler Loan Guarantee Board said the application had not yet been received, but that provisions were being made to receive it Tuesday night.

Revised operating and financing plans were submitted with the application, Chrysler said. Although those documents are not public, Chrysler has said it seeks a $600 million wage freeze from its employees through September 1982, a price freeze from suppliers, and conversion of bank loans into preferred stock. 

An executive at Chrysler headquarters familiar with the plans said they projected cash savings of $1.2 billion to $1.5 billion in 1981 and reduced losses, or increased profits, in 1981 of "almost a billion," $500 million in 1982 and $300 million spread over 1983, 1984 and 1985.

What the phrase "almost a billion" meant was not clear. Chrysler last week put the figure at $660 million. The executive asked not to be quoted by name.

Chrysler has already receeived $800 million of the $1.5 billion in guaranteed loans authorized by Congress. To get more the loan guarantee board must approve the application and certify that the company is likely to survive.

Chrysler says its plan projects a 1981 profit if its cars -- domestic and imported -- get 9.0 percent of a 9.6-million market next year. This forecast represented a lowering of Chrysler's goals, but it was not known by how much. Chrysler's imported and domestic models have accounted for 8.65 percent of a 1980 market of about 9.0 million.

The United Auto Workers union has agreed to negotiate about a wage freeze, but only after it confers with the loan board. UAW officials have scheduled a meeting with Treasury Secretary G. William Miller, chairman of the loan board, on Jan. 6, after his return from a vacation in the Bahamas.

The executive said the automaker was expecting conditional approval -- the condition being that all parties involved fall in line with Chrysler's latest plans. He said he thought conditional approval could come even while Miller was vacationing because the secretary could cast his vote, one of three on the board, by telephone.

"It makes a lot of sense" to get conditional approval before new union negotiations begin, he argued, because then the board "can tell the UAW there's a piece missing -- yours."

Even after final approval, 15 days must pass before Chrysler can receive any money. The company says it can probably last through January. UAW members could not ratify any further concessions until after they return to their plants Jan. 12.

UAW President Douglas A. Fraser has urged Chrysler to seek the entire $700 million remaining in guarantees but the executive said, "You don't want to get more than you need because you have to pay interest on the money."

Bank Loans Up In Latest Week


SECTION: Business News


LENGTH: 246 words


DATELINE: NEW YORK


Loans at major New York City banks continued to rise in the latest reporting week, despite near-record interest rates, figures released Friday by the New York Federal Reserve Bank showed.

Nationwide figures on bank loans and on the nation's money supply, usually released on Friday, were delayed until early next week, a spokesman for the Federal Reserve said. 

The New York figures showed commercial and industrial loans at major New York City banks rose $358 million to $51.306 billion in the week ended Dec. 17. That compared with an increase of $737 million in the previous week.

The latest report was the 17th time in the past 21 weeks that loan demand at New York banks has shown an increase. The net gain in that period totals more than $6.5 billion.

The latest increase in loans, while smaller than the previous week, came at a time when the prime lending rate was running at 20 percent to 21 percent at banks across the nation.

The prime, which is the rate banks charge on loans to their most creditworthy corporate customers, later climbed to a record 21.5 percent. Chase Manhattan, the nation's third largest bank, and several smaller banks early this week lowered their prime rates 20.5 percent.

The New York Fed's report said its latest weekly loan figures exclude bankers acceptances of commercial paper.

The report also said that for the week ended Wednesday, the Federal Reserve decreased its holdings of government securities by $3.815 billion.
